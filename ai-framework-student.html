AI Competency Framework v1.0

Student AI Competency Framework

Age-appropriate learning outcomes for AI literacy across year groups. Three age bands (Primary, Lower Secondary, Upper Secondary) across four strands. AI literacy is not a separate subject — subject teachers share responsibility for developing student AI competency through their teaching.

Eduversal AI Competency Framework v1.0 · Companion: Classroom Activities · Teacher Framework

How to use this page

The matrix below shows what students should be able to do at each age band, across four strands of AI literacy. Click any cell to read the verbatim descriptor. Subject teachers use these as learning outcomes when planning AI-literacy activities in their classes.

Cambridge International's Feb 2026 Primary + Lower Secondary Digital Literacy curriculum refresh aligns closely with these outcomes. Indonesia's Permendikdasmen 13/2025 (Koding & AI elective) is curriculum-scope; this framework is subject-integrated.

Strand 1
Understanding AI
Identifies everyday AI examples (voice assistants, recommendations). Knows AI is human-made, follows patterns, doesn't understand like humans.

Conceptual understanding of machine learning. Distinguishes generative / analytical / predictive AI. Knows hallucination, bias, lack of common sense.

Strand 2
Using AI Responsibly
Asks teacher or parent before using AI tools. Knows AI information should be checked. Understands using AI to do the work is dishonest.
Primary · S2
student.primary.strand2
Uses approved AI tools with teacher guidance. Follows school AI policy. Writes clear prompts. Verifies AI output. Understands academic integrity and disclosure.
LS · S2
student.lower_secondary.strand2
Uses AI effectively across academic tasks. Sophisticated judgement on when AI is appropriate. Navigates Cambridge integrity rules confidently. Documents AI use.
US · S2
student.upper_secondary.strand2
Strand 3
Evaluating AI Outputs
Knows AI makes mistakes even when confident. Identifies obvious errors with teacher help. Checks AI information against books, teachers, trusted sites.
Primary · S3
student.primary.strand3
Verifies AI output against credible sources. Identifies factual mistakes, false citations, anachronisms. Spots cultural bias, stereotypes, one-sided perspectives.
LS · S3
student.lower_secondary.strand3
Rigorous evaluation of AI outputs (accuracy, depth, bias, fit-for-purpose). Identifies subtle errors. Synthesises AI with multiple sources. Critiques AI claims.
US · S3
student.upper_secondary.strand3
Strand 4
AI and Society
Discusses ways AI helps people daily (accessibility, safety, medicine). Understands people have different views. Discusses fairness and kindness in tech.
Primary · S4
student.primary.strand4
Discusses AI benefits and risks (employment, privacy, information quality). Understands AI bias. Considers multiple perspectives. Knows own responsibilities as a user.
LS · S4
student.lower_secondary.strand4
Analyses complex AI social issues with nuance. Understands governance, accountability, transparency. Evaluates ethical frameworks. Prepares for AI-transformed society.
US · S4
student.upper_secondary.strand4
Integration
How to teach this
Naturally embedded into computing, science, English, PSHE. Direct AI tool use limited to teacher-led demonstrations.
Primary · Guide
student.primary.integration
Across-subject integration + explicit computing/digital literacy. Guided AI use with clear learning purposes and responsible-practice instruction.
LS · Guide
student.lower_secondary.integration
Deep integration across curriculum + explicit technical courses. Extended essays and major assessments include AI-use expectations and demonstrate own thinking.
US · Guide
